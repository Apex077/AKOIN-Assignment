# Logo Design Rationale — Institute of Digital Risk

## Design Choices

**Colour.** The palette of **orange (#ff6600), black (#0d0d0d) and white (#ffffff)** was chosen for both brand and semantic reasons. Orange conveys urgency, alertness and energy, which are qualities that resonate in the risk and cyber-security domain. 

The near-black ground suggests depth, seriousness and technical precision. White provides a clean contrast and ensures legibility at all sizes. Together they meet WCAG AA contrast requirements, which allows for better accessibility and a distinct look from the generic blue-heavy palettes common in financial services.

**Symbolism.** The mark is an **isometric cube built from four distinct faces**: the top face is a bold orange, representing risk as something visible, present and requiring immediate attention. Beneath it, a bright white horizontal band suggests the clarity and transparency that rigorous risk practice delivers. The left face is near-black, evoking depth, consequence and the weight of high-stakes environments. The right face carries no fill, receding naturally into the dark background to create a convincing sense of three-dimensional depth. Together, the four faces communicate the layered nature of digital risk and what is exposed, what is understood, and what remains in shadow.

**Typography.** The wordmark pairs a lightweight tracking of "INSTITUTE OF" (regular weight, generous letter-spacing) with a heavy-weight "DIGITAL **RISK**" — where "RISK" is set in orange to anchor the eye and telegraph the organisation's focus instantly. Inter was selected for its geometric sans-serif construction, excellent screen legibility at both display and small sizes, and its neutral authority that suits an academic-industry institution.

**Legibility at small sizes.** The icon-only variant strips back to the three-faced cube alone; the orange top face remains the dominant element at favicon size (16×16 px) and in compressed mobile headers, ensuring instant brand recognition without the wordmark.
